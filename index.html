About

I am a graduate research assistant at The University of Texas in Austin. I collaborate with Dr. Hans Hofmann to study functional variability of single neurons and to enhance transdiciplinary training in interdisciplinary approaches to research. My research focuses on on understanding transcriptional variability in neurons with single neuron resolution. My educational efforts aim to improve training in computational approaches for bench scientist. My outreach activities promote the sharing of ideas across typical disciplinary boundaries.

Publications

My research spans a variety of topics and utilizes multiple approaches to better understand biological phenomena. I have been envolved in multiple collaborative projects that led to publications and have bolstered my own independent research projects.

For the most up-to-date listing of my publications, visit my Google Scholar page. You can also view my publications with Altmetric scores here.

Or, check out this commentary I published in PNAS on gene and genome evolution.

Blogs

I have written a handful of guest blog posts for PLOS Neuroscience, BEACON, and Software Carpentry on topics related to academic research, education, and mentoring. All of these blogs can be found on Medium at https://medium.com/@raynamharris.

Teaching

In 2015, I became a certified Software Carpentry Instructor and have since taught UNIX to a wide audience of aspiring bioinformatians. I also serve on the Mentoring and the Assessment Subcommittees.I have learned more about teaching and bioinformatics from the Software Carpentry community than through any formal education.

In the summers, I am a course developer for the Neural Systems & Behavior at the Marine Biological Laboratory. My mission is to conduct integrative research and develop new educational exercises to enhance the course curricula. Here's the molecular lab manual I created for teaching neural gene expression profiling techniques. Here's a 30 minute video of me giving a lecture titled Time and money: Techniques for neural gene expression profiling.

I was an organic chemistry lab teaching specialists full-time from 2006-2009. In 2012 I was a teaching assistant for Problems in Modern Biology in with Dr. Ruth Buskirk.

Community

I love bringing people together to talk science, especially when I see that these interactions foster new relationships and projects. Working with the The Center for Computational Biology and Bioinformatics, I have many opportunities to organize networking events, yearly symposia, and training opportunities for researchers using computational approaches to solve biological problems.
